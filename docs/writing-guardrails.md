# Writing Guardrails — Anti-AI Patterns

These are patterns that make writing identifiable as AI-generated. They apply to all contexts: proposals, LinkedIn, X, cold outreach. The source is a comprehensive field guide to AI writing tells (Wikipedia's Signs of AI Writing), distilled to what matters for Anik's specific outputs.

The core problem with all of these: LLMs regress to the statistical mean. They produce language that sounds vaguely impressive and could apply to anything. Anik's writing is the opposite — specific, earned, particular. These patterns are the enemy of that.

---

## 1. Banned Words

These words appear in AI output at statistically abnormal rates post-2023. One or two may be coincidental. Clusters are a near-certain tell. Never use them.

**The list:**
- `additionally` (especially to open a sentence)
- `align with` / `aligns with`
- `crucial`
- `delve` / `delve into`
- `emphasizing` / `emphasize` (as a closing -ing phrase)
- `enduring`
- `enhance` / `enhancing`
- `fostering`
- `garner`
- `highlight` / `highlights` (as a verb meaning "shows" or "demonstrates")
- `interplay`
- `intricate` / `intricacies`
- `key` (as a vague adjective — "key role", "key moment", "key takeaway")
- `landscape` (as an abstract noun — "competitive landscape", "evolving landscape")
- `pivotal`
- `showcase` / `showcasing`
- `tapestry` (almost always figurative and meaningless)
- `testament` ("is a testament to")
- `underscore` / `underscores` (as a verb)
- `valuable` (as a vague intensifier — "valuable insights", "valuable experience")
- `vibrant`
- `robust` (as a generic intensifier)
- `leverage` (as a verb — also an Anik personal pet peeve)
- `synergy` / `synergies`
- `groundbreaking` (figurative use)
- `renowned`
- `profound` (as a vague intensifier)

## 2. Banned Phrases & Constructions

### Significance inflation
AI puffs up ordinary things into world-historical importance. Every statement becomes a "pivotal moment" or "reflects broader trends." In proposals and LinkedIn copy, this reads as empty hype.

Never write:
- "X stands as a testament to..."
- "This marks a pivotal moment in..."
- "X reflects broader trends in..."
- "Setting the stage for..."
- "X represents a significant shift toward..."
- "This underscores the importance of..."
- "X has left an indelible mark on..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "As we navigate [abstract noun]..."
- "In an ever-evolving landscape..."

### Superficial analysis tacked on with -ing phrases
AI attaches significance-claims to the end of factual sentences using present participles. These add nothing — they just assert that what was just said is important.

Never write:
- "...highlighting its significance in the industry."
- "...underscoring the importance of this approach."
- "...contributing to the broader conversation around..."
- "...reflecting the company's commitment to..."
- "...fostering a culture of innovation."
- "...ensuring long-term success."
- Any sentence that ends with an -ing clause that could be deleted without losing any information.

### Vague authority / weasel wording
AI invents authority it doesn't have by attributing claims to unnamed experts.

Never write:
- "Industry reports suggest..."
- "Experts argue that..."
- "Observers have noted..."
- "Some critics argue..."
- "It is widely believed that..."
- "Many founders find that..."
- "Research shows..." (without a specific source)

### Promotional / puffery language
AI writes about companies and products the way bad press releases do.

Never write:
- "Metaborong boasts a team of..."
- "Our cutting-edge solutions..."
- "We are committed to delivering world-class..."
- "Our innovative approach to..."
- "Best-in-class..." / "State-of-the-art..."
- "We are excited to announce..."
- "We are pleased to offer..."

### Negative parallelisms (overused "not X, but Y" structures)
AI uses these to sound balanced and thoughtful. In clusters, they read as formulaic.

Use sparingly — maximum once per piece:
- "Not only... but also..."
- "It's not just about X, it's about Y."
- "This isn't X — it's Y."

Note: Anik's proposals do use contrast constructions ("You're not just buying an app. You're buying 4 weeks to find out...") — these work because they're earned and specific. The AI version is generic and unearned.

### False ranges
AI uses "from X to Y" constructions to sound comprehensive when no real scale exists.

Never write:
- "From startups to enterprises..."
- "From ideation to execution..."
- "From strategy to implementation..."
(Unless a real spectrum is being described with specific meaning at each end.)

### "Serves as" instead of "is"
AI avoids simple copulatives ("is", "are") in favour of fancier constructions that add no meaning.

Prefer:
- "Metaborong **is** a product studio" — not "Metaborong **serves as** a product studio"
- "This **is** the problem" — not "This **represents** the core challenge"
- "The team **has** 20 people" — not "The team **boasts** 20 people"

## 3. Structural Patterns to Avoid

### The "Challenges and Future Outlook" section
AI ends pieces with a formulaic section that follows this pattern: "Despite its [positives], X faces challenges including... However, with [vague initiatives], X is well-positioned for the future."

Never do this. If there are real challenges worth discussing, name them specifically and say something real about them.

### The rule of three used to pad
AI clusters ideas into threes even when two or four would be more accurate. "Metaborong delivers speed, quality, and innovation" — this is padding. Say the one thing that's actually true.

### The outline-as-content structure
AI creates content that reads like a table of contents with flesh on it: headers like "Background", "Key Features", "Benefits", "Conclusion." In proposals and LinkedIn posts, this kills voice entirely.

### Opening with context before the point
AI warms up before it says anything. Anik doesn't.

Never open with:
- "In the rapidly evolving world of AI and Web3..."
- "As founders and builders, we often face the challenge of..."
- "It goes without saying that..."
- Any sentence whose job is to establish context before making a point.

## 4. Formatting Anti-patterns

### Excessive bold
Bold should mark one critical phrase per section — the single thing most worth isolating. AI bolds every key term in a list, every section subheading, every phrase it wants to seem important. If everything is bold, nothing is.

### Emoji as decoration
Never use emoji as bullet point decorations or section header markers (🧠, 🚀, ✅). If an emoji is used at all, it serves a specific rhythmic purpose — and even then, sparingly.

### Excessive headers in short content
A 300-word LinkedIn post does not need four H2 headers. A cold outreach email does not need any. Headers signal document structure. Most of Anik's writing is not a document.

### Bullet-point prose
AI converts naturally flowing arguments into bulleted lists. If the items have a logical connection — if order matters, if one leads to another — they should be prose, not bullets. Bullets are for genuine lists: deliverables, specs, options.

---

## The Underlying Principle

AI writing regresses to the mean. It produces language that sounds like it could be about anything. The tell is not any single word — it's the absence of specificity, the inflation of vague importance, the avoidance of direct simple statements.

Anik's writing is the opposite: specific claim, specific number, specific person, specific outcome. The guardrail is always: could this sentence have been written about a different company in a different industry? If yes, rewrite it.
